# Where this is going

This is the north star. It states *where the model is heading* and *why it exists at all* — the larger thing the current machinery is one manifestation of. The constitution ([operating-model.md](operating-model.md)) states what the model **is today**; this document states what it is **becoming**. It is deliberately written as *emerging*, not settled: the specifics below will change, and this file is expected to change with them. Read it as direction, not doctrine.

## The thing we're actually building

We are building a **model of how to work — and increasingly how to run a business — in the age of AI.** Not a project-management method. A management model for a world where a third kind of worker exists.

For most of history there have been two ways to get something done: **delegate it to a human**, or **automate it with a tool**. Those have opposite management shapes. You delegate to a person with judgment, context, and autonomy — and you manage the *relationship*: intent, trust, review. You automate with a tool that is fast, literal, and deterministic — and you manage the *specification*: inputs, outputs, correctness.

**AI sits in the liminal space between those two.** It is not a person and not a tool. It has enough judgment to be delegated to, and enough speed and machine-nativeness to be automated with — at the same time. Managing it well is therefore neither pure delegation nor pure automation; it is a **new discipline that borrows from both and collapses into neither.** That discipline is what this operating model is trying to name and make repeatable.

## Why it's called "Graveyard" right now

The **Graveyard Shift** — autonomous agents doing real work off durable, visible state while you sleep — is the biggest, sharpest idea in the model *so far*. So it names the whole thing today. That naming is a snapshot, not the ceiling.

This has happened before, deliberately: **OS Manager was called "Skills Manager"** when managing skills was what it did — and it has since grown past skills into a broader operating surface, so the name grew with it. Expect the same here. "Graveyard Operating Model" is the name of the **current biggest idea**, and the model will grow beyond it. More ideas about how to work with AI are coming; the graveyard shift is the first load-bearing one, not the last.

**Corollary for anyone reading later:** don't mistake the current name for the boundary of the thing. The graveyard mechanism is *a* manifestation of the model, not its full extent.

## The trajectory: from tech work to the business

Today the model is expressed mostly in **technical** terms — issues, PRs, shifts, reviews, externalization. That is where it started because that is where AI's leverage was most obvious and most measurable. But the model is not about code. It is heading toward **how we build a business that means something.**

The unifying frame is this: **we are in the idea business.** Ideas are the actual product. Everything else is a *manifestation* of an idea into a substrate the world can use:

- **Software** is a manifestation of ideas — ideas made executable, operating at machine speed.
- **Content** is a manifestation of ideas — ideas made legible, so a human can absorb and be moved by them.
- **Services** are a manifestation of ideas — ideas made into an ongoing relationship.

**AI lives precisely at the intersection of these.** It can act on machines at speed (the software axis), it can produce and shape content (the content axis), and it can stand in a service relationship. That intersection is exactly the liminal space above — and it is why AI is the right substrate to build an idea business on: it is the one worker that operates across all three manifestations at once.

This is why the model **must** cover content, not just code. The essay/mental-model pipeline is as canonical here as the PR pipeline — both are the same act (capture an idea, move it through a durable, visible pipeline, manifest it) pointed at different substrates. A model that managed only the software manifestation would be managing a fraction of the actual business.

## Ideas in, business out

The deeper loop the model is reaching for: **capture ideas, and have those ideas reflect back into the business.** An idea surfaced in a conversation, a shift, a transcript, an essay draft — should not evaporate. It should land in a durable, visible substrate, get worked, and come back as something the business runs on: a shipped feature, a published essay, a decision, a new way of working.

Notice that **the substrate is itself content.** The documents, the markdown, the decision logs, the briefs — the very artifacts this model produces to manage itself — are ideas made durable and readable. The medium and the message are the same material. When we externalize knowledge into git and Notion, we are not just doing bookkeeping; we are practicing the exact "idea → durable manifestation" move that the business is *for*, on the business itself.

That is the whole thing, in one line: **a way of working where AI, sitting between delegation and automation, turns ideas into their manifestations — software, content, services — off durable state that compounds instead of evaporating.**

## What this document is not

- **Not a spec.** Nothing here overrides the constitution or the skills. When this vision and the current canon disagree on a concrete rule, the canon wins *for now* — and the disagreement is a signal to evolve one of them.
- **Not finished.** The claim in the opening is real: this is understood partially and will be understood better. This file is the current best articulation of the direction, to be corrected as the model actually grows.
- **Not the naming.** Don't rename anything off this document. When the model genuinely outgrows "Graveyard," that will be its own deliberate decision (as Skills Manager → OS Manager was), recorded in [DECISIONS.md](DECISIONS.md).
